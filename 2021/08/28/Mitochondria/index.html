Mitochondria

Mitochondria (mitochondrion) is a two-layer membrane-coated organelle found in most eukaryotic cells, with a diameter of about 0.5 to 10 microns. Talking about the origin of mitochondria, one theory proposed by Lynn is called Endosymbiosis Hypothesis. This hypothesis assumes that mitochondria originated from mitochondrial ancestors that were engulfed by another gram-negative bacterium which is capable of tricarboxylic acid cycle and electron transport. Evidence that supports the endosymbiosis theory has been found to include: Mitochondria have an inner membrane and have their own DNA, which is similar in shape to bacterial circular DNA; Mitochondria’s DNA encodes specific proteins expressed in mitochondria; Mitochondria’s genetic code and the genetic codes of proteobacteria are more similar; mitochondrial ribosomes are similar in size and structure to bacterial ribosomes but are different from those of eukaryotes.
